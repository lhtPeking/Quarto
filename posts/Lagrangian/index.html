1 Motivation

If you are an undergraduate student majoring in physics, I bet that when you heard about the definition of Lagrangian \(L=T-V\) and the principle of least action for the first time, you felt like a fly rubbing its head off. But no worries, I will derive “Lagrangian and Action” from the first principle in this blog and show you how the symmetry is tightly associated with conservation, namely Noether’s Theorem.

2 The introduction of generalized coordinates

Newton’s Laws become extremely cumbersome when dealing with systems containing many particles and constraints. We need to write down Newton’s Second Law for every particle:

\[ m_i \ddot{\vec{r_i}}=\vec F_i + \vec N_i \tag{1}\]

where \(\vec N_i\) represents constraint force acting on the \(i\)-th particle, which means it will not contribute to any work if the particle only moves in the allowed direction. Most of the time, it is hard to identify force \(\vec N_i\) since the constraint could be very complicated, and this is where Lagrangian Mechanics comes into play.

Suppose we have \(N\) particles with \(k\) constraint equations in a 3D space, the degrees of freedom of this system is \(3N-k\). Naturally, we could introduce \(3N-k\) new coordinates \(\{q_1, q_2, ..., q_{3N-k}\}\) to match these degrees of freedom, and these coordinates are called “generalized coordinates”.

Therefore, the position of the i-th particle could be expressed using the general coordinates that occupied all the spatial freedom of the system:

\[ \vec{r}_i=\vec{r}_i(q_1,q_2,\ldots,q_{3N-k},t) \tag{2}\]

And its differential form could be written as:

\[ d\vec{r}_i = \frac{\partial \vec{r}_i}{\partial q_1}dq_1 + \frac{\partial \vec{r}_i}{\partial q_2}dq_2 +\cdots+ \frac{\partial \vec{r}_i}{\partial q_{3N-k}}dq_{3N-k} + \frac{\partial \vec{r}_i}{\partial t}dt \tag{3}\]

Divide both sides by \(dt\) we obtain the velocity representation under generalized coordinates:

\[ \vec{v}_i = \frac{d\vec{r}_i}{dt} = \frac{\partial \vec{r}_i}{\partial q_1}\dot{q}_1 + \frac{\partial \vec{r}_i}{\partial q_2}\dot{q}_2 +\cdots+ \frac{\partial \vec{r}_i}{\partial q_{3N-k}}\dot{q}_{3N-k} + \frac{\partial \vec{r}_i}{\partial t} \tag{4}\]

Besides, there are two important identity equations we need to prove in this section. For consistency, you can skip this part and head directly to the next section. If you are interested in the mathematical details, you can come back to see the proof.

Theorem 1 (Displacement-Velocity Relationship) This identity shows that the partial derivative of the particle position with respect to a generalized coordinate is equal to the partial derivative of the particle velocity with respect to the corresponding generalized velocity.

\[ \frac{\partial \vec v_i}{\partial \dot q_j} = \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \]

Proof. As we stated before, the velocity of a particle could be expressed as:

\[ \vec v_i = \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial q_j}\dot q_j + \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial t} \]

Taking the partial derivative of both sides with respect to \(\dot q_j\), we obtain:

\[ \frac{\partial \vec v_i}{\partial \dot q_j} = \frac{\partial}{\partial \dot q_j} \left( \sum_{l=1}^{3N-k} \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial q_l}\dot q_l + \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial t} \right) \]

Since \(q_l\) and \(\dot q_l\) are independent generalized variables (not contradictory to the description of \(\vec{r_i}\) only needs \(\{q_1, q_2, ...,q_{3N-k}\}\)), only the term containing \(\dot q_j\) remains:

\[ \frac{\partial \vec v_i}{\partial \dot q_j} = \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \]

Therefore, the identity is proved.

Theorem 2 (Order-irrelevance Principle) This identity shows that the partial derivative with respect to a generalized coordinate and the total time derivative commute when applied to the particle position.

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \right) = \frac{\partial\vec v_i}{\partial q_j} \]

Proof. The derivative of \(\vec r_i\) with respect to \(q_j\) is itself a function of all generalized coordinates and time:

\[ \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial q_j} = \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial q_j} (q_1,q_2,\dots,q_{3N-k},t) \]

Therefore, taking the total time derivative gives:

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \right) = \sum_{l=1}^{3N-k} \frac{\partial^2 \vec r_i} {\partial q_j\partial q_l} \dot q_l + \frac{\partial^2 \vec r_i} {\partial q_j\partial t} \]

On the other hand, taking the partial derivative of \(\vec v_i\) with respect to \(q_j\) gives:

\[ \frac{\partial \vec v_i}{\partial q_j} = \frac{\partial}{\partial q_j} \left( \sum_{l=1}^{3N-k} \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial q_l}\dot q_l + \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial t} \right) \]

Because \(q_j\) and \(\dot q_l\) are treated as independent variables, we obtain:

\[ \frac{\partial \vec v_i}{\partial q_j} = \sum_{l=1}^{3N-k} \frac{\partial^2 \vec r_i} {\partial q_j\partial q_l} \dot q_l + \frac{\partial^2 \vec r_i} {\partial q_j\partial t} \]

Therefore,

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \right) = \frac{\partial\vec v_i}{\partial q_j} \]

and the identity is proved.

3 Analyze the particle system in a “bundle”

As we stated before, the power of Lagrangian mechanics lies in its holistic view of the system, instead of analyzing the system particle-by-particle in Newton’s mechanics. In this section, I will show you how the Lagrangian analyzes the many-particle system in a “bundle”.

Again, Newton’s Second Law for particle \(i\):

\[ m_i \ddot{\vec{r_i}}=\vec F_i + \vec N_i \tag{5}\]

If we consider a “virtual displacement” \(\delta \vec{r_i}\) within a timescale \(dt=0\), constraint force \(\vec N_i\) doesn’t contribute to any work: \[ \vec N_i\cdot\delta\vec r_i=0 \tag{6}\]

Substitue \(\vec N_i\) with \(m_i \ddot{\vec{r_i}}-\vec F_i\), we obtain the D’Alembert Principle for a single particle: \[ \left(m_i\ddot{\vec r}_i-\vec F_i\right) \cdot \delta\vec r_i=0 \tag{7}\]

We could furthur decompose the virtual displacement \(\delta \vec{r_i}\) on to the generalized coordinates. Given that \(d\vec r_i=\sum_j\frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial q_j}dq_j+\frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial t}dt\) and \(dt=0\), we have:

\[ \delta\vec r_i = \sum_j \frac{\partial \vec r_i}{\partial q_j}\delta q_j \tag{8}\]

Subtitute this into D’Alembert Principle, we obtain its general coordinated form: \[ \sum_j m_i\ddot{\vec r}_i \cdot \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \delta q_j - \sum_j \vec F_i \cdot \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \delta q_j =0 \tag{9}\]

The next step we’re gonna take is where the rubber hits the road. We are going to sum over all the particles to get a comprehensive equation for the whole system, you will see what this brings us in a minute. For now we just sum over \(i\):

\[ \sum_j \left( \sum_i m_i\ddot{\vec r}_i \cdot \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \right) \delta q_j - \sum_j \left( \sum_i \vec F_i \cdot \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \right) \delta q_j =0 \tag{10}\]

The equation above could be re-organized into the following form just by switching the order of the two summations: \[ \sum_j \left[ \sum_i m_i\ddot{\vec r}_i \cdot \frac{{\partial\vec r}_i}{\partial q_j} - \sum_i \vec F_i \cdot \frac{{\partial\vec r}_i}{\partial q_j} \right] \delta q_j =0 \tag{11}\]

Note that both \(\sum_i m_i\ddot{\vec r}_i\cdot\frac{{\partial\vec r}_i}{\partial q_j}\) and \(\sum_i\vec F_i\cdot\frac{{\partial\vec r}_i}{\partial q_j}\) are quantities indexed by \(j\) instead of by \(i\), and both terms have clear physical interpretations.

The virtual work of the whole system could be expressed as: \[ \delta W= \sum_{i=1}^{N} \vec F_i\cdot\delta\vec r_i= \sum_{i=1}^{N} \vec F_i\cdot \left( \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \delta q_j \right) = \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} \left( \sum_{i=1}^{N} \vec F_i\cdot \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \right) \delta q_j \tag{12}\] Therefore, the work on the \(j\)-th direction could be represented as: \[ \left( \sum_{i=1}^{N} \vec F_i\cdot \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \right) \delta q_j \tag{13}\]

The displacement on the \(j\)-th direction is \(\delta q_j\), therefore the term \(\sum_i\vec F_i\cdot\frac{{\partial\vec r}_i}{\partial q_j}\) could be interpreted as the “generalized force” on the \(j\)-th direction. Similarly, the term \(\sum_i m_i\ddot{\vec r}_i\cdot\frac{{\partial\vec r}_i}{\partial q_j}\) could be interpreted as the “generalized inertia force” on the \(j\)-th direction.

Thereby, we could give them new symbols to define them more clearly:

Definition 1 (Generalized Force & Generalized Inertia Force) Here we use \(Q_j\) and \(I_j\) to represent generalized force and generalized inertia force on the \(j\)-th direction, respectively.

\[ Q_j := \sum_i\vec F_i\cdot\frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \qquad I_j := \sum_i m_i\ddot{\vec r}_i\cdot\frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \]

And thus the D’Alembert’s principle in terms of generalized coordinates could be expressed as:

\[ \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} \left(I_j-Q_j\right)\delta q_j=0, \tag{14}\]

In the following section, I’m gonna establish the relationship between \(Q_j\) and the system potential ennergy \(V\) and the relationship between \(I_j\) and the system kinetic ennergy \(T\), and derive the Lagrangian from the first principle.

4 The Lagrangian and the Principle of Least Action

Theorem 3 (Generalized Inertial Term and Kinetic Energy) Let the total kinetic energy of the particle system be

\[ T = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i\vec v_i\cdot\vec v_i. \]

Then the generalized inertial term satisfies

\[ I_j = \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot q_j} \right) - \frac{\partial T}{\partial q_j}. \]

Proof. Starting from the kinetic energy,

\[ T = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i\vec v_i\cdot\vec v_i, \]

Taking the partial derivative of the kinetic energy with respect to \(\dot q_j\), we obtain

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot q_j} &= \frac{\partial}{\partial \dot q_j} \left[ \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i \left( v_{ix}^{\,2} + v_{iy}^{\,2} + v_{iz}^{\,2} \right) \right] \\[4pt] &= \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i \left( 2v_{ix}\frac{\partial v_{ix}}{\partial \dot q_j} + 2v_{iy}\frac{\partial v_{iy}}{\partial \dot q_j} + 2v_{iz}\frac{\partial v_{iz}}{\partial \dot q_j} \right) \\[4pt] &= \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i \left( v_{ix}\frac{\partial v_{ix}}{\partial \dot q_j} + v_{iy}\frac{\partial v_{iy}}{\partial \dot q_j} + v_{iz}\frac{\partial v_{iz}}{\partial \dot q_j} \right) \\[4pt] &= \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i\vec v_i\cdot \frac{\partial\vec v_i}{\partial \dot q_j}. \end{aligned} \]

Using the position–velocity derivative identity,

\[ \frac{\partial\vec v_i}{\partial\dot q_j} = \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j}, \]

we obtain

\[ \frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot q_j} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i\vec v_i\cdot \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j}. \]

Taking the total time derivative gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot q_j} \right) &= \frac{d}{dt} \left( \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i\vec v_i\cdot \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \right)\\ &= \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i\ddot{\vec r}_i\cdot \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} + \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i\vec v_i\cdot \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \right). \end{aligned} \]

The first term is precisely \(I_j\). Moreover, using the commutation identity

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j} \right) = \frac{\partial\vec v_i}{\partial q_j}, \]

the second term becomes

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i\vec v_i\cdot \frac{\partial\vec v_i}{\partial q_j} = \frac{\partial T}{\partial q_j}. \]

Therefore,

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot q_j} \right) = I_j+\frac{\partial T}{\partial q_j}, \]

and hence

\[ I_j = \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot q_j} \right) - \frac{\partial T}{\partial q_j}. \]

For a conservative system, the force acting on particle \(i\) can be written as

\[ \vec F_i=-\nabla_i V, \]

where \(V\) is the potential energy of the entire system. Therefore,

\[ \begin{aligned} Q_j &= \sum_{i=1}^{N} \vec F_i\cdot \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j}\\ &= -\sum_{i=1}^{N} \nabla_i V\cdot \frac{\partial\vec r_i}{\partial q_j}\\ &= -\frac{\partial V}{\partial q_j}. \end{aligned} \]

Thus, we have the relationship:

Theorem 4 (Generalized Force Term and Potential Energy) \[ Q_j=-\frac{\partial V}{\partial q_j}. \]

Substituting the expressions for \(I_j\) and \(Q_j\) into D’Alembert’s principle gives

\[ \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} \left[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot q_j} \right) - \frac{\partial T}{\partial q_j} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial q_j} \right] \delta q_j = 0. \]

The generalized virtual displacements \(\{\delta q_1,\delta q_2,\dots,\delta q_{3N-k}\}\) are independent. Let \(\{\delta q_1,\delta q_2, ..., \delta q_{j-1}, \delta q_{j+1},\delta q_{3N-k}\}\) equal to \(0\), we obtain:

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot q_j} \right) - \frac{\partial T}{\partial q_j} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial q_j} = 0 \]

For an ordinary conservative system, the potential energy \(V\) does not depend on the generalized velocities:

\[ \frac{\partial V}{\partial\dot q_j}=0 \]

We may write the equation above as:

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial (T-V)}{\partial\dot q_j} \right) - \frac{\partial (T-V)}{\partial q_j} = 0 \]

Therefore, we could define the Lagrangian naturally as:

\[ L(q,\dot q,t):=T(q,\dot q,t)-V(q,t) \]

The equation of motion then becomes

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q_j} \right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_j} = 0 \qquad j=1,2,\dots,3N-k. \tag{15}\]

These are the Euler–Lagrange equations.

For a trajectory

\[ q(t) = \left( q_1(t),q_2(t),\dots,q_{3N-k}(t) \right), \]

we define the action functional as the integration of Lagrangian over time:

\[ S[q] := \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L\left( t, q_1(t),\dots,q_{3N-k}(t), \dot q_1(t),\dots,\dot q_{3N-k}(t) \right)\,dt. \tag{16}\]

Here, the action \(S\) is not an ordinary function of a finite number of variables. Instead, it is a functional.

To examine whether a given trajectory is physical, we introduce a nearby family of trajectories:

\[ q_j^{(\varepsilon)}(t) = q_j(t)+\varepsilon\eta_j(t), \]

where \(\varepsilon\) is a small parameter and \(\eta_j(t)\) represents an arbitrary variation of the trajectory. The endpoints are fixed, i.e.\(\eta_j(t_1)=\eta_j(t_2)=0\).

The action evaluated along this perturbed trajectory becomes a function of \(\varepsilon\):

\[ S(\varepsilon) := S[q+\varepsilon\eta]. \]

Using conclusions from functional analysis, a trajectory that satisfies Euler–Lagrange equation will give rise to a local extrema of the integration. In this case:

\[ \frac{dS(\varepsilon)}{d\varepsilon}=0 \]

Therefore we have the theorem:

Theorem 5 (Principle of Least Action) The Lagrangian \(L=T-V\) of a system naturally satisfies Euler–Lagrange equations, which means the action \(S\), the integration of \(L\) over time, is an extrema, usually a minimum.

5 Noether’s Theorem

The power of the Lagrangian formulation becomes especially clear when the system possesses a continuous symmetry.

A symmetry is a continuous transformation of the generalized coordinates—or of time—under which the action remains unchanged. Noether’s theorem states that every such continuous symmetry produces a conserved quantity.

Define the generalized momentum conjugate to \(q_j\) by

\[ p_j := \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q_j}. \]

Theorem 6 (Noether’s Theorem) Suppose the generalized coordinates undergo an infinitesimal transformation

\[ q_j \longrightarrow q_j+\varepsilon\xi_j(q,t), \]

while time remains fixed. If the Lagrangian is invariant to first order,

\[ \delta L=0, \]

then the quantity

\[ J = \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} p_j\xi_j \]

is conserved:

\[ \frac{dJ}{dt}=0. \]

Proof. Under the infinitesimal transformation,

\[ \delta q_j=\varepsilon\xi_j \]

and

\[ \delta\dot q_j = \varepsilon\frac{d\xi_j}{dt}. \]

The corresponding change in the Lagrangian is

\[ \delta L = \varepsilon \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_j}\xi_j + \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q_j} \frac{d\xi_j}{dt} \right). \]

Using the Euler–Lagrange equations,

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_j} = \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q_j} \right) = \dot p_j, \]

we obtain

\[ \begin{aligned} \delta L &= \varepsilon \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} \left( \dot p_j\xi_j + p_j\dot\xi_j \right)\\ &= \varepsilon \frac{d}{dt} \left( \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} p_j\xi_j \right). \end{aligned} \]

If the transformation is a symmetry, then \(\delta L=0\). Therefore,

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} p_j\xi_j \right) = 0. \]

Hence,

\[ J = \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} p_j\xi_j \]

is conserved.

The theorem above is the simplest fixed-time form of Noether’s theorem. The more general theorem also allows transformations of time and changes in the Lagrangian by a total time derivative.

5.1 Time-Translation Symmetry and Energy Conservation

Suppose the Lagrangian has no explicit dependence on time:

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial t}=0. \]

The total derivative of the Lagrangian is

\[ \frac{dL}{dt} = \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_j}\dot q_j + \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q_j}\ddot q_j \right) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial t}. \]

Using

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_j} = \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q_j} \right), \]

we find

\[ \frac{dL}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt} \left( \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q_j}\dot q_j \right) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial t}. \]

Therefore,

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q_j}\dot q_j - L \right) = -\frac{\partial L}{\partial t}. \]

When \(\partial L/\partial t=0\),

\[ \boxed{ E = \sum_{j=1}^{3N-k} p_j\dot q_j-L = \text{constant} }. \tag{17}\]

For a natural mechanical system with

\[ L=T-V, \]

where \(T\) is quadratic in the generalized velocities and \(V\) is velocity-independent,

\[ \sum_j p_j\dot q_j=2T. \]

Hence,

\[ E=2T-(T-V)=T+V, \]

which is the conservation of mechanical energy.

5.2 Spatial-Translation Symmetry and Momentum Conservation

Suppose the Lagrangian does not depend on a generalized coordinate \(q_j\):

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial q_j}=0. \]

Such a coordinate is called a cyclic coordinate or ignorable coordinate. The Euler–Lagrange equation immediately gives

\[ \frac{d}{dt} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q_j} \right) = 0. \]

Therefore,

\[ \boxed{ p_j = \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q_j} = \text{constant} }. \]

When \(q_j\) represents a Cartesian translation, \(p_j\) is the corresponding component of linear momentum. Thus, spatial-translation symmetry leads to momentum conservation.

5.3 Rotational Symmetry and Angular-Momentum Conservation

Consider an infinitesimal rotation about a fixed unit vector \(\hat n\). The position of particle \(i\) changes by

\[ \delta\vec r_i = \varepsilon\, \hat n\times\vec r_i. \]

The corresponding Noether charge is

\[ \begin{aligned} J &= \sum_{i=1}^{N} \vec p_i\cdot \left( \hat n\times\vec r_i \right)\\ &= \hat n\cdot \sum_{i=1}^{N} \vec r_i\times\vec p_i. \end{aligned} \]

Therefore, rotational invariance implies that

\[ \boxed{ \vec L_{\mathrm{angular}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \vec r_i\times\vec p_i } \]

is conserved.

In summary,

\[ \begin{aligned} \text{time-translation symmetry} &\Longrightarrow \text{energy conservation},\\ \text{spatial-translation symmetry} &\Longrightarrow \text{linear-momentum conservation},\\ \text{rotational symmetry} &\Longrightarrow \text{angular-momentum conservation}. \end{aligned} \]
